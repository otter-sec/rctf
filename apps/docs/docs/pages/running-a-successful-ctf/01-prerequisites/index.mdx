# Prerequisites [Organizational and logistical requirements]

So you want to run a CTF! Whether you're a university club hosting your first event, an established team launching an annual competition, or a company organizing an internal security challenge, this guide will walk you through the essential steps to plan, build, and execute a successful CTF competition.

Running a CTF is a significant undertaking that requires coordination across challenge development, infrastructure, sponsorship, and communication. The following sections outline the organizational and logistical foundations you'll need to establish before technical details are covered.

## Organizational structure

A successful CTF requires a dedicated team with clearly defined roles and responsibilities. The following hats are commonly worn:
- **Organizers** are responsible for overall coordination, timeline management, and decision-making,
- **Challenge authors** are responsible for designing, implementing, and testing challenges.
- **Infrastructure leads** are responsible for platform deployment, challenge hosting, and monitoring.
- **Communications leads** are responsible for social media, announcements, and sponsor relations.
- **Support team** is responsible for responding to competitor tickets.

:::tip[Team size]
For a typical 24-48 hour CTF with 20-30 challenges, a team of 5-10 organizers is generally sufficient. Larger events may require additional personnel.
:::

### Timeline

It is advisable to begin preparations at least 2-3 months before the event. The following is a suggested timeline:

| Timeframe | Tasks |
|-----------|-------|
| T-3 months | Finalize date, register on CTFtime, ideate challenges, reach out to sponsors |
| T-2 months | Challenge development begins, infrastructure planning |
| T-1 month | Challenge testing, platform setup |
| T-2 weeks | Internal playtesting, challenge revisions, infrastructure testing |
| T-1 week | Public announcement, registration open, Discord server preparation |

## CTFtime

[CTFtime](https://ctftime.org) serves as the central hub for tracking CTF events, participating teams, and related information. It is the most effective way to promote a CTF to the broader community and to verify that a planned date does not conflict with other events.

To list your CTF on CTFtime, you need to complete two steps:
1. [Create your team](https://ctftime.org/team/new/). A team can both partake and organize CTFs.
2. [Register your event](https://ctftime.org/event/mail/). This creates an entry for your recurring CTF series, such as "My CTF." Event registration must be reviewed and manually approved by the CTFtime team, so be sure to start this process early, well before your event date, to ensure your listing appears in time.
3. [Add an edition of your event](https://ctftime.org/event/new/). Each instance of your CTF (for example, "My CTF 2026" held on April 1st, 2026) is considered an edition. Once your event is registered and approved, you can add a new edition for each specific competition date.

Following the conclusion of the CTF, it is highly recommended to upload the final scoreboard to the event page. This enables participating teams to receive points, which contribute to their global ranking. The longer an event has been organized (i.e., the more editions it has), [the higher weight](https://ctftime.org/faq/#weight-vote) it may achieve based on competitor votes.

:::info
Additional information regarding CTF organization is available on CTFtime's [organizer resources page](https://ctftime.org/for-organizers/).
:::

## Homepage

The homepage of a CTF is the first point of contact for participants. It should be designed to convey information regarding event timing, rules, prizes, sponsors, and other relevant information.

### Rules

Clear rules are essential for fair competition and participant expectations. The following is an example ruleset:

> - There is no team limit.\*
> - Do not attack or interfere with other teams, the competition infrastructure, or other competitors.
> - Do not share flags, solutions, or hints with other teams.
> - Do not discuss challenges with other teams until the CTF is over.
> - Do not attempt to brute-force flags or flag submission endpoints.
> - Do not use automated tools or scanners against competition infrastructure.
> - Each player may only be a member of one team. Do not register multiple accounts.
> - Tiebreakers are resolved by the timestamp of the last solve that brought the team to their final score.
> - Violating any of the above rules may result in disqualification at the discretion of the organizers.

:::info
\*Team size limits are typically unenforceable. rCTF uses a shared "team token" authentication model rather than individual user accounts. All team members log in using the same token, meaning there is no concept of individual users, but rather a collective team identity.
:::

### Sponsorships and prizes

While not strictly mandatory, the majority of competitions choose to provide prizes for the top three teams. Additionally, some events award prizes for outstanding write-ups, as these serve as valuable learning resources for the community. To obtain such funding, it is often necessary to obtain sponsorship from companies, organizations, or individuals.

The following are potential sponsors to consider:

- [OtterSec](https://osec.io/ctf)

- [GCP credits for CTF infrastructure by Google](https://goo.gle/ctfsponsorship)

- Additional sponsors may be identified by reviewing the sponsor lists of [upcoming](https://ctftime.org/event/list/upcoming) and [past](https://ctftime.org/event/list/past) CTF events.

The following are potential benefits that your sponsor may ask for in return for sponsorship:
- Logo placement on the CTF homepage and merchandise, if applicable
- Shoutouts on social media and announcement channels
- Special usage of particular frameworks or tools for challenges or infrastructure
- Guest authorship for challenges
- If your event is held in a physical location, the ability to speak at the event or run a booth

:::warning
It is advisable to begin outreach to potential sponsors well in advance, as companies may require time to evaluate and confirm sponsorship arrangements.
:::

## Communication

### Discord setup

The majority of CTFs utilize Discord as the primary communication platform for announcements, support, and post-event discussion.

A typical Discord server configuration for a CTF includes the following channels:

| Channel | Purpose |
|---------|---------|
| `#rules` | Discord and CTF-specific rules |
| `#announcements` | Registration status, CTF start/end times, challenge updates, and feedback requests |
| `#first-bloods` | Recognition of teams who achieved the first solve on a challenge |
| `#support` | Support ticket creation for issues such as broken challenges (e.g., via [Ticket Tool](https://tickettool.xyz/)) |
| `#general` | General discussion |
| `#looking-for-team` | Team formation for participants seeking teammates |
| `#web`/`#rev`/`#pwn`/`#crypto`/`#misc` | Category-specific discussion channels, if desired |

It is common to receive a high volume of support requests from participants. For this reason, it is significantly more efficient and manageable to handle such inquiries within a centralized server environment rather than through individual direct messages. Typical support requests may include issues related to the CTF platform, malfunctioning challenges, incorrect flags, or requests for hints.

### Hints

It is strongly advised to implement and enforce a strict no-hint policy, as providing hints to individual teams may be perceived as conferring an unfair advantage in what is ultimately a competitive environment. Should a participant inquire about a particular challenge, organizers should limit their response to verifying that the challenge infrastructure and corresponding solution function as intended, typically by using a reference exploit. No additional information regarding the challenge should be disclosed to competitors.

If hints or clarifications become necessary, always share them publicly in the `#announcements` channel. In a standard 48-hour CTF, it's common to release hints for unsolved challenges around the 24-hour mark. When considering a hint, ask participants to open a support ticket outlining their current progress; the challenge author should then determine whether a hint is appropriate and, if so, compose and share it publicly.
